01 — Color Palette

Pale stone, deep ink.
No accent color.

The product is the color. Leather provides every warm tone the brand needs — saddle tan, cognac, oiled brown. The palette itself stays neutral so nothing competes with the goods.

Saddle
Reserved — leather tones only

!

Rule: there is no brand accent color. Where the old site used fire-engine red for buttons, links, and badges, this system uses ink-on-stone contrast and weight instead. If a single warm highlight is ever needed (a sale badge, an in-stock dot), pull it directly from a real leather swatch, not from a UI color picker.

02 — Typography

One family.
Weight does the work.

Manrope, end to end. Light for scale and elegance, Bold for emphasis and structure, Mono for anything numeric. No serif, no script — restraint is the point.

Headlines

Light weight (200–300) at large size, tight letter-spacing (-2% to -4%). Bold weight reserved for one or two emphasis words per headline only.

Body copy

Regular weight, no letter-spacing adjustment, line-height 1.6. Keep paragraphs under 60 characters per line for readability.

Labels & data

Geist Mono for anything that reads as a spec, SKU, lot number, or price — it signals "fact," not marketing copy.

03 — Logo & Lockups

The badge is a stamp.
The wordmark is the brand.

Your existing badge mark (scalloped shield, slab serif, "Est. 2024") carries real handmade warmth — keep it, but treat it as a stamp: packaging, hang tags, social avatar, leather burn-stamp reference. For the website and digital presence, lead with a plain-set wordmark in Manrope so it matches the Stone & Ink system.

Clearspace: maintain padding equal to the cap-height of "RAWHIDE" on all sides. Minimum digital width: 120px. Minimum print width: 1in.

Don't

Mixing marks

Don't run the badge mark and the wordmark side by side in the same lockup — pick one per surface.

Recoloring the badge

Don't tint the badge mark in cognac, red, or any accent color — it stays pure ink or pure stone, full contrast only.

Condensing the wordmark

Don't compress or stretch the wordmark to fit a space — resize proportionally or shorten the lockup to "Rawhide City."

04 — Voice & Tone

Plain-spoken.
Earned, not advertised.

A firefighter built this brand. The copy should sound like someone who's good at their craft talking about it directly — not a marketing department talking about a firefighter.

Direct
Understated
Specific
Earned, not boastful
Zero hype words

✓ Do

  • Lead with concrete facts: material, weight, who made it, where.
  • Use short sentences. Let the product carry the weight.
  • Reference the trade plainly — "long shift," "the bench," "the rig" — without overexplaining it.
  • Let imperfection read as proof of hand-work, not apologize for it.

✕ Don't

  • Use words like "premium," "elevated," "curated," "artisanal."
  • Lean on exclamation points or urgency ("Don't miss out!").
  • Over-explain the firefighter angle — state it once, let it sit.
  • Write headlines that could belong to any leather brand. Be specific to this one.
